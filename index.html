Before you watch

This episode picks up in 1945 — the end of World War II. The United States has just defeated Nazi Germany and Imperial Japan. It controls more than half the world’s oil and two-thirds of its gold. 140 million people are ready to build.

As you watch, hold onto the two questions from your station work: Did American power actually deliver on its promises? And: Who paid the cost — and were they in the room when the decisions were made?

Write while you watch. Don’t wait until the end. The questions follow the episode in order.

Question types →
recall Answer is in the episode

0:00 – 2:30
The Greatest Moment of Collective Inebriation
What the U.S. had at the end of WWII — and what it felt like
👁  The episode opens with statistics. Pay attention — they set up everything that follows.
1. According to the episode’s opening, what fraction of the world’s oil and gold did the U.S. control in 1945? recall
2. One narrator calls 1945 “the greatest moment of collective inebriation in all of American history.” What does “inebriation” actually mean, and why might he choose that word instead of “celebration” or “pride”? analyze

Think about what inebriation implies about judgment and clarity, not just happiness.

3. The average American family earned 15 times more than European families at this point. What does that gap tell you about the condition of Europe after WWII — not just the success of America? analyze

2:30 – 10:30
Getting America Moving — The Interstate Highway System
Eisenhower’s vision, the engineering scale, and who got left behind
👁  The episode connects the highway system to earlier American transportation. Watch for that pattern — rivers, canals, railroads. Each era solves the same problem differently.
Key fact
Before the interstate system, only half of U.S. roads were paved. Eisenhower’s cross-country drive as a young soldier took 62 days. After the interstates: 4 days.
4. What personal experience gave Eisenhower his motivation to build the interstate highway system? recall
5. The episode identifies a common theme across rivers, the Erie Canal, and the railroads. What is it? recall

The narrator uses almost the exact same phrase to describe all three.

6. The episode says the highway system was “built with manual labor and sheer grit” — $129 billion and 2.4 billion man-hours. Who do you think actually did that labor? The episode doesn’t say, but what do you know? analyze
7. The episode describes “death by interstate” — towns bypassed and hollowed out when the highways went around them. Who benefited from the interstate system? Who paid a cost? connect

Add this to your thinking about the cost ledger from station work. Who was in the room when Eisenhower made this decision?

10:30 – 20:00
Levittown and the Birth of Suburban America
330 babies per hour, mass-produced housing, and who the suburbs were built for
👁  Listen for the phrase “Ford production line of house building” and what it implies about how Americans thought about home, family, and efficiency.
8. How many new babies were being born per hour in 1946, and how many homes were built over the following decade? recall
9. Levittown, New York: 17,000 identical homes, one built every 16 minutes. The episode compares this to Ford’s assembly line. What are the advantages of building houses this way? What might be lost? analyze
10. By 1960, 60% of Americans owned their own homes — up from 40% in 1940. One narrator calls this “the spread of affluence.” What questions would you want answered before accepting that statistic as evidence of broad prosperity? analyze

Think about who could get a mortgage in the 1950s. Who could actually buy in Levittown?

11. The episode says cities like New York lost 2 million people to the suburbs. How does suburban growth connect to what was happening to inner-city communities — particularly Black communities — in postwar America? connect

The episode doesn’t say this explicitly. Use what you know.

12. Name one specific technology mentioned in this segment (other than the interstate) that changed where or how Americans could live. recall

20:00 – 27:30
Apollo 11 and the Final Frontier
400,000 Americans, 8 years, 30 seconds of fuel — and what it all meant
👁  Pay attention to how close to failure this actually was. The episode makes it feel triumphant — but the margins were razor-thin.
13. How many Americans worked directly on the Apollo 11 mission? Name at least three different types of workers the episode mentions. recall
14. How much fuel remained when the lunar module landed? What would have happened if the engine burn to enter lunar orbit had failed? recall
15. A narrator says: “You have to remember what we had come through leading up to that summer night in 1969.” What had America come through? Why did the moon landing matter emotionally — not just scientifically? analyze

The narrator gives you two specific examples from the late 1960s.

16. The episode traces a line: Wright Brothers → two World Wars → jet age → Apollo. What does that sequence suggest about the relationship between war and technological progress? Is that a troubling connection, a neutral one, or something else? analyze

27:30 – 35:40
The Fault Line That Shamed the Nation
Military desegregation, the March on Washington, and the Civil Rights Act
👁  Notice how the episode frames the civil rights movement as the “second civil war.” Also notice the connection it draws to Black soldiers in WWII — that link is important.
Timeline to track
1619: Africans arrive in Virginia  ·  1861–65: Civil War  ·  1865: emancipation → then segregation  ·  1948: military desegregated  ·  1963: March on Washington  ·  1964: Civil Rights Act
17. The episode says Black soldiers serving in WWII were “the beginning of the modern civil rights movement.” What is the argument? Why would military service lead to civil rights activism? analyze

Think about what it means to fight and die for a country’s freedom when you don’t fully have your own.

18. When was the U.S. military desegregated? The episode quotes a military officer: “We don’t care if you’re black. We don’t care if you’re white. The only thing we care about is performance.” What does that quote suggest about how the military saw itself compared to civilian society? recall
19. The episode says King “held a mirror up to the face of all Americans” and pointed back to the Declaration of Independence. In your own words: what was the gap King was naming between America’s founding promise and its 1963 reality? analyze
20. The Civil Rights Act passed in 1964 — 99 years after the Civil War ended. One narrator says “we waited 100 years after the Civil War to take that issue up again.” Why do you think it took that long? What forces kept it from happening sooner? connect

Use what you know from earlier in the year and what the episode shows about power, fear, and who decides.

35:40 – 43:20
Red Alert — The Bomb, the Soviets, and Duck and Cover
The Manhattan Project, the arms race, and the fear that reshaped daily American life
👁  This is your direct on-ramp to the Cuban Missile Crisis simulation. Pay close attention to how Americans felt during the Cold War, not just what happened.
The number that defines this era
From 1940 to 1996, the United States spent $5.5 trillion on nuclear weapons — nearly $20,000 for every man, woman, and child in America.
21. When did the Soviet Union successfully test its own atomic bomb, and why did this change the nature of the threat Americans felt? recall
22. The episode describes duck-and-cover drills. Children were told to hide under a desk so “debris from glass blowing in from the windows when the atomic bomb went off downtown wouldn’t hurt us.” What does the existence of these drills tell you about how the government was communicating — or not communicating honestly — with the public about nuclear war? analyze

Think honestly: would hiding under a desk help if a nuclear bomb hit downtown?

23. Name both specific Cold War incidents the episode mentions that brought fear to a peak. Give the year for each. recall

One involves a spy plane. One involves Cuba.

24. The episode describes a culture of “self-imposed conformity” in the 1950s. It gives a specific three-part contrast between how Americans defined themselves against communism. What were those three contrasts? What does it tell you about how fear shapes identity? analyze

The narrator gives you “If the communists are X, Americans are Y” — three times in a row.

25. The episode says the moon landing “unites America” — then immediately cuts to: “America is held back. There’s a fault line that shames the nation. Race.” What does that editorial cut tell you about what the episode is actually arguing? analyze
After the episode — one last question

The episode is called “Superpower.” By almost any measure — military, economic, technological — the United States in 1945 was exactly that.

But you’ve now seen what that power looked like up close: towns killed by the interstate, families who couldn’t buy in Levittown, soldiers who fought for a country that segregated their units, children practicing hiding under desks in case of nuclear war.

In two or three sentences: does the word “Superpower” feel like a celebration, a description, a warning, or something else — and why?
